\section{PRISMA Framework}
\label{sec:prisma}
The importance of documenting the research process to demonstrate the exhaustiveness of the literature review conducted has been emphasized in prior work. A robust literature review comprises querying databases for relevant literature using keywords as well as performing backward and forward searches based on these findings. Because of the plethora of literature on the subject matter, systematically including and excluding existing research must be made as transparent as possible. Describing which existing research was included and excluded is vital for the credibility of a literature analysis. \cite{brocke2009reconstructing, webster2002analyzing}

To support in this, the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) framework was applied. The goal of PRISMA is to show why the review was done, how studies were identified and considered, and what was found. \cite{page2021prisma}

Literature was primarily identified using the Google Scholar search engine. The search process involved defining and iteratively refining relevant keywords clustered around the main themes of this thesis. Forward and backward citation tracking was also applied to the extracted sources. Search terms included EAM, LLMs, RAG, and Conversational Agents. In addition to manual search methods, a number of additional sources were identified through prompted searches using ChatGPT, which served as a supplementary discovery mechanism. \cite{openai_chatgpt_2023} A flowchart of the applied PRISMA framework can be seen in figure \ref{fig:prisma}.

Setting up criteria to filter out excessive literature is necessary to leave only the relevant pieces. Academic, peer-reviewed sources were the main filtering criteria. This was to ensure the academic integrity of the research process and results. The research's focus was then laid on the categories defined by the keyword search terms. The sources found were required to provide value to this thesis. Given the practical, implementation-oriented nature of this thesis, particular focus was placed on design- and system-oriented contributions rather than purely theoretical or analytical studies. A further crucial filtering criterion applied was the publishing date, which is especially relevant for research on LLMs and conversational agents, as models such as ChatGPT only adopted widespread use in late 2022 \cite{buchmann2024large}, meaning research before then is less likely to be applicable to this thesis. Metadata of a published paper, such as the number of times it has been cited by further papers, may also be an indication of how well grounded the source is within its domain. For example, a paper cited by thousands of further papers is likely to be a key paper within its domain. A paper cited by little to no further papers is to be critically considered. Non-academic sources were kept to a minimum.

Applying the above filters, the papers found were initially skimmed to assess whether they might be relevant to this research. This involved reviewing abstracts and key contributions without yet analyzing the full content in detail. This allows papers that do not support the thesis's goal to be quickly filtered out. Afterward, the remaining papers were read more thoroughly by analyzing the applied methods, results, and contributions.

By applying the PRISMA framework as described above, the number of sources was reduced from an initial set of \textasciitilde 120 identified records to 75 sources after initial screening and finally to a subset of relevant academic sources forming the core literature base of this thesis.

In addition to the systematically identified literature, supplementary sources such as technical documentation and online resources were included during the development and writing process. These were not part of the PRISMA-based search but were included to support specific aspects of the developed prototype.

In total, 45 sources were used throughout this thesis. Of these, 21 were identified via Google Scholar, 7 via AI-assisted search, 4 via forward and backward citation tracking, and 13 from other sources such as technical documentation and online resources.

While the PRISMA process ensured a comprehensive identification of relevant literature, not all identified sources are equally suited for detailed analysis within this chapter. From the final set of 45 sources used throughout this thesis, only 7 of the most relevant papers and 3 related projects were selected for in-depth discussion within this chapter. These were chosen for their direct relevance to the research objective, particularly with regard to implemented systems and approaches that combine LLMs, RAGs, and structured knowledge representations. The remaining sources are used as supporting literature throughout the thesis, providing theoretical background and contextual grounding.

% Jung, Fraunholz 2021: Masterclass Enterprise Architecture Management
% Key points: Supplementing an LLM with RAG
% EAM Overview
Jung and Fraunholz 2021 \cite{jung2021masterclass} lay foundational work for many concepts within the realm of EAM and identify recurring challenges associated with understanding, documenting, and managing enterprise architectures and application landscapes in practice.

One common challenge addressed throughout the textbook is that large, complex application landscapes are difficult to keep an overview of. In extreme cases, this can even lead to an enterprise lacking a complete overview of the applications in use, which severely limits transparency and hinders decision-making based on the data at hand. Another common hurdle arises from uncontrolled landscape growth, for example, through a merger or acquisition. In such scenarios, previously independent landscapes are fused into a single landscape, often leading to redundant systems, overlapping functionality, and unnecessary costs. Even in cases where the application landscape may not be overly complex, it may still suffer from poor documentation. This lack of reliable documentation hinders enterprise architects' ability to understand application purposes, dependencies, and the capabilities covered.

%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------



%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Jung, Fraunholz 2021: Masterclass Enterprise Architecture Management
% Key points: Common stakeholder challenges in EAM
Jung (2019) \cite{jung2019purpose} conducted eight interviews in which enterprise architects, consultants, and decision-makers in the German logistics industry identified concerns that  enterprise architecture management is expected to address in practice. The results highlight that stakeholders address recurring topics, such as providing information about applications, optimizing application landscapes, and reducing the number of applications to save costs. These concerns provide a practical starting point for examining how enterprise architecture data can be structured, queried, and analyzed to support questions about existing landscapes.

%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


The above expressed challenges can be summarized as a lack of transparency within the application landscape, and support the thought that these challenges can be addressed with the assistance of AI. Recurring tasks, such as retrieving simple information about an application landscape, can be challenging when done manually due to the complex, heterogeneous nature of large landscapes. Such hurdles may be especially difficult to overcome for junior enterprise architects, as they are not yet able to navigate the landscape. The challenges addressed by the previous two sources suggest they are predestined to be supported by AI-assisted tooling. This underlines the case for LLM-assisted enterprise architecture management.

However, LLMs are notorious for hallucinating, meaning that incorrect information is spewed out while looking as though it could be factually correct. This is a major downside when interacting with an LLM, especially if expert knowledge in a specific domain is required.


%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Zhao et al. 2024: Retrieval augmented generation (rag) and beyond: A comprehensive survey on how to make your llms use external data more wisely
% Key points: Supplementing an LLM with RAG and categorizing levels of complexity
Zhao et al. (2024) \cite{zhao2024retrieval} investigate how to overcome this issue by supplementing an LLM with a RAG-based solution. Augmenting the context given to an LLM comes with the advantage that the data is up to date, aligned with domain expert knowledge, reduces hallucination, and the data supplied in the context explains the answers generated.

A key challenge the authors identified when developing a RAG-based LLM system is enabling the LLM to comprehend the varied forms of data required in the applied domain. In the case of the research at hand, this means that a central challenge when developing the RAG-based LLM system will be to structure and query the data so that the LLM has the right context to respond to the user with factual accuracy. It is also mentioned that making a system transparent helps increase trust and reliability in it.

To tackle this challenge, the authors identify four levels of complexity in information retrieval. The levels are as follows: level 1, for less complex queries; level 2, for moderately complex queries; level 3, for complex queries; and level 4, for complex information retrieval operations.

% Four levels of query-complexity
\begin{enumerate}[label=Level \arabic*, leftmargin=*]
	\item Explicit Facts: Require no reasoning and are facts found directly in the data.
	\item Implicit Facts: May require common reasoning or logical deductions based on the data.
	\item Interpretable Rationales: Require a grasp of the factual content as well as domain-specific knowledge, which is integral to the data's context.
	\item Hidden Rationales: Require that more information than that which is being asked is considered to generate the answer.
\end{enumerate}

While queries at levels 1 and 2 are rather straightforward and only require the system to retrieve the relevant facts, queries at levels 3 and 4 require the system to learn and apply rationales that go beyond the data itself. The challenge of query tasks that require rationale can be overcome through in-context learning. The authors describe this as an LLM's ability to infer hidden rationales from relevant examples within the context of the prompt.

These categories will be relevant again later in chapter \ref{ch:method} as these are also applicable within the domain of enterprise architecture management. Each question posed to the system within this research can be assigned to one of these four categories.

However, categorizing questions does not yet address how information from a knowledge graph can be retrieved. This is especially challenging, as each question by a user contains a certain level of complexity that needs to be covered by the generated query. How can this challenge be overcome?

%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------



%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Wan et al. 2025: Prompting large language models based on semantic schema for text-to-Cypher transformation towards domain Q\&A
% Key points: Text-to-cypher
Wan et al. (2025) \cite{wan2025prompting} investigate how LLMs can effectively be employed in order to generate Cypher queries and retrieve domain-specific information from a knowledge graph, in order to supplement LLM-generated answers by being more relevant to the domain at hand. The authors propose a text-to-Cypher approach that leverages a lightweight, semantic schema derived from the domain ontology. By injecting the relevant parts of this schema at query runtime, the LLM can be guided toward generating syntactically correct and semantically aligned Cypher queries without requiring model retraining and without overloading the LLM's context. The injected schema context enables the LLM to generate domain-aligned answers grounded in the structure of the knowledge graph.

This illustrates that a text-to-Cypher approach offers key advantages, such as aligning an LLM with domain-specific information and enabling domain-aligned reasoning without retraining. This will be used within this thesis. Additionally, this allows an LLM to remain up to date with a changing knowledge graph by dynamically incorporating the updated schema information at runtime. By exposing access to the knowledge graph through natural language inputs, the proposed approach lowers the barrier for non-technical users to interact with and extract information from it, allowing for the democratization of the information within it. The research by Wan et al. further reinforces the suitability of a graph-based solution for the research at hand, as enterprise architecture involves highly interconnected data and requires reasoning across various entities and their relationships. 
%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------



Combining the research by Zhao et al. (2024) and Wan et al. (2025), it becomes clear that a RAG-based system LLM should be able to assess the complexity of a user's request and generate queries accordingly. While Zhao et al. (2024) provide a high-level framework for understanding the complexity of the task given by the user, their work remains agnostic to concrete query-generation techniques required to interact with the knowledge graph. This gap is addressed by Wan et al. (2025) through their text-to-Cypher approach.

Now, the question arises: how can a RAG-based LLM system be able to retrieve the relevant information from the knowledge graph to answer the user's prompt with factual correctness?



%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Chen et al. 2025: GRIL: Knowledge Graph Retrieval-Integrated Learning with Large Language Models
% Key points: Schema
Chen et al. (2025) \cite{chen2025gril} propose a graph-retrieval approach that is tightly integrated with an LLM by allowing it to iteratively retrieve and refine relevant subgraphs from a knowledge graph for downstream reasoning. This works by identifying seed entities within the graph based on the user's prompt. A graph retriever then iteratively expands from these seed entities along promising relations while pruning irrelevant nodes and edges. The extracted relevant subgraphs are then given to the LLM as context for reasoning. This multi-hop retrieval strategy allows the system to retrieve complex relational information necessary to answer more complex user queries.

In contrast to this multi-hop approach, simpler approaches rely on the LLM-as-retriever paradigm. In such approaches, the LLM performs a one-time extraction based on what the LLM considers relevant within the graph. Chen et al. explicitly criticize this LLM-as-retriever strategy. They argue that this does not allow the subgraphs and their intricacies to be fully exploited. Moreover, it requires multiple LLM calls to explore different parts of the graph, leading to complex queries and scalability issues when applied to large knowledge graphs.

%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


So far, it has only been discussed how to retrieve information from a knowledge graph. However, it has not yet been discussed how to persist information within it. Preparing and storing data in a graph representation poses its own challenges. The method for correctly breaking down data into nodes and edges depends on the source data. For example, converting a semi-structured textbook will require a different approach than converting an existing, structured application landscape. How can the challenge of creating a knowledge graph be overcome?


%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Laurenzi et al. 2024: An LLM-aided enterprise knowledge graph (EKG) engineering process
% Key points: Umwandlung von Daten in eine GraphDB
Laurenzi et al. (2024) \cite{laurenzi2024llm} address this challenge by proposing a six-step process for creating a knowledge graph with the assistance of LLMs. The six-step cycle is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Understand what types of questions the knowledge graph will have to be able to answer, as defined in the previous research by Zhao et al. (2024).
	\item Construct the knowledge graph's schema.
	\item Extract the knowledge from the data source and map it to the ontology of the knowledge graph.
	\item Validate the mapping between the data source and the knowledge graph.
	\item Add the extracted data to the knowledge graph.
	\item Update and maintain the knowledge graph.
\end{enumerate}

Their research takes an LLM-based approach to each of the six steps. For example, in step 1, an LLM was used in order to generate competency questions in natural language. In step 5, an LLM was used in order to generate queries based on natural language input. Within their research, ChatGPT achieved the best results. Following the example in step 1, the LLM generated well-tailored competency questions for the knowledge graph. For step 5, it was also able to generate queries, with only minor corrections necessary for successful knowledge extraction. However, the authors note that this step requires a certain awareness of the LLM and the underlying ontology schema, enabling the user to understand what information is being queried and how this may be improved. Query results can then be validated against the actual knowledge graph instance. This awareness is beneficial when working with LLMs and knowledge graphs.

%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------



The results of this study are beneficial for the research at hand, as it details how to set up a knowledge graph with the assistance of LLMs. This will be relevant again later. However, while the work by Laurenzi et al. (2024) demonstrates how LLMs can assist in the creation of a knowledge graph, this process requires a human in the loop who is aware of the ontology. Moreover, this approach may be flexible, but it is not scalable. In many cases, when dealing with enterprise architectures, the data is already structured in architecture diagrams and can be exported from the respective modeling tool into structured formats such as XML. This raises the question of how an automated approach can be taken in cases where the artifacts exist and only require systematic parsing in order to be added to the knowledge graph, without the assistance of an LLM.



%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Smajevic and Bork 2021: From conceptual models to knowledge graphs: a generic model transformation platform
% Key points: Umwandlung von Daten in eine GraphDB
Smajevic and Bork (2021) \cite{smajevic2021conceptual} introduce a generic, end-to-end platform that enables automated transformation of conceptual models into knowledge graphs. The authors define a cloud-based platform that allows predefined models exported from Archi in XML to be uploaded. The uploaded model is then transformed and added to their Neo4j graph database. However, the authors do not provide detailed information on how this transformation is conducted. For the research at hand, the results show that an automated approach for transforming common export formats is a feasible solution for generating a knowledge graph using a preexisting conceptual model.
%%%%%%%%%% -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------


The above two studies by Laurenzi et al. (2024) and Smajevic et al. (2021) both exemplify two different approaches to creating knowledge graphs. While Laurenzi et al. (2024) propose an LLM-based, hands-on approach that offers flexibility in creating a knowledge graph from the ground up, Smajevic et al. (2021) offer a generic solution to transform an existing model into a knowledge graph in an automated fashion. Both approaches have advantages and disadvantages and will be relevant in chapter \ref{ch:Discussion}.

\section{Related Projects}
\label{sec:intro:projects}
Given that the research at hand concerns the development of a prototypical RAG-based LLM chatbot, it is worthwhile to briefly consider related projects. The goal of this section is not to provide a detailed description, but to position existing projects in relation to the prototype developed in this thesis. An overview of the related projects is shown in Table \ref{tab:related_projects}.

Edge et al. (2025) \cite{MsGraphRAGPaper} introduce GraphRAG\footnote{GraphRAG on GitHub: \url{https://github.com/microsoft/graphrag}}, a graph-based extension of RAG that addresses the limitation of traditional RAG systems in answering global, sensemaking questions over large document collections. The approach constructs a knowledge graph from the source data and generates hierarchical summaries that enable reasoning over the entire dataset rather than relying on a small set of retrieved documents. While this improves the comprehensiveness and diversity of answers, the method relies on precomputed summarization rather than precise, user-driven information extraction. As a result, it lacks direct query control and does not address domain-specific requirements such as enterprise architecture, limiting its applicability to the context of this thesis.

Dragon1\footnote{Dragon1: \url{https://www.dragon1.com/}} \cite{Dragon1BusinessCase} is a SaaS platform for enterprise architecture management that focuses on modeling, analyzing, and communicating architectural artifacts through interactive visualizations. It emphasizes stakeholder alignment and transparency by providing a centralized repository and visual access to architectural data. However, it relies on manual modeling and exploration and does not provide AI-supported querying or automated access to architectural knowledge.

Opoku et al. (2025)\cite{opoku2025rag} propose DO-RAG, a domain-specific RAG framework that combines knowledge graph generation and semantic retrieval to improve answer accuracy within specific domains. While the approach enhances domain alignment and reduces hallucinations through its four-step pipeline, it relies on a largely opaque retrieval process and does not provide transparent, schema-aware query generation. As a result, it does not fully address the need for controllable and inspectable information retrieval in enterprise architecture contexts.

All three approaches highlight different aspects relevant to this thesis. While GraphRAG demonstrates how knowledge graphs and LLMs can be combined to enable reasoning over large datasets, it relies on precomputed summarization and lacks precise, user-driven information extraction and domain-specific adaptation. In contrast, Dragon1 focuses on structuring and visualizing enterprise architecture data to improve transparency, but lacks intelligent retrieval and query mechanisms. DO-RAG, on the other hand, introduces domain-specific retrieval to improve answer quality but relies on an opaque retrieval process and does not provide transparent, schema-aware query generation. Similarly, recent advances in LLM-based text-to-Cypher approaches enable natural-language access to knowledge graphs but often fall short in terms of transparency in query generation.

This thesis builds upon these findings by proposing a schema-aware, transparent, and query-driven approach tailored to enterprise architecture data, combining the flexibility of LLMs with the required domain knowledge in EAM contexts. The next chapters examine the applied Action Research and the development of the prototype.
