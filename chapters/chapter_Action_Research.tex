\section{Action Research Design}
\label{sec:action_research_design}
Action Research was applied in order to gain scientific value from the development of the prototype. The advantage of this research method is that it supports the development process for information systems. According to Baskerville (1999) \cite{baskerville1999investigating}, all types of Action Research share the following four characteristics: an orientation towards developing, a focus on a specific problem, an iterative process, and collaboration amongst participants. This is applicable to the work at hand because a prototype was developed to address a specific problem, in an exploratory manner, with support from industry experts.

% What cyclical steps are
Cyclical steps are central to the concept of Action Research, which contains an iterative process consisting of five steps within a single cycle. Other sources, such as Cornish et al. (2023) \cite{cornish2023participatory}, propose variations with fewer (usually three) steps per cycle; however, these models also boil down to the same concepts. Across the literature, Action Research cycles follow the same structure: planning what to do in the new cycle, taking action, and evaluating the outcome of the completed cycle before moving on to the next one. \cite{baskerville1999investigating, cornish2023participatory}

% Steps in the cycle
The paper at hand applied a cycle using the following five steps according to Baskerville (1999) \cite{baskerville1999investigating}: diagnosing, action planning, action taking, evaluating, and specifying learning. The reason this research uses five cycles instead of three is to provide a more detailed description of the development process. This five-step cycle, including the preliminary and subsequent steps, built the basis of the conducted Action Research.

The following list describes the goal of each of the five steps \cite{baskerville1999investigating}:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Diagnosing: Identifies the primary problems the organization needs to address.
	\item Action planning: Researchers and practitioners collaborate to determine the next steps to address the identified pain points.
	\item Action taking: The actual implementation of the planned actions.
	\item Evaluating: The collaborative evaluation of the outcomes of the action-taking step. Evaluation includes determining whether the theoretical effects of the action were realized and whether this relieved the problems. The output of this step is generally the input for the next cycle.
	\item Specifying learnings: The learnings of the cycle must be documented and applied to the next cycle.
\end{enumerate}

% Cycle duration
Within this research, each cycle lasted between two and three weeks. The consulted sources do not mention how long a single cycle should last. However, a timeframe of two to three weeks per cycle was deemed reasonable for the development of \prototype{} because status updates were held at the end of each cycle, and with the given time for each cycle, there was enough progress to evaluate in these status update meetings. In total, four development cycles were conducted.

% Participatory Action Research
Action Research typically leaves the main theorizing up to the researcher. However, an extended form of Action Research, called Participatory Action Research, goes a step further by creating a more collaborative environment between the researcher and additional participants. Instead of leaving theorizing to the researcher, new information and ideas are generated together with the other participants, giving both parties an active role. This is beneficial because the other participants often have theoretical and practical knowledge of the subject matter being worked on. \cite{baskerville1999investigating} From here on out, the term Participatory Action Research will be used interchangeably with Action Research.

\section{Action Research Setup}
\label{sec:action_research_setup}

% Domain
This research was focused on the domain of EAM. In particular, this study primarily addressed application landscapes, business capability maps, business object models, and the relationships between these architectural artifacts. These artifacts are commonly used to document an enterprise's application landscapes and to align its IT with its strategic business objectives, as described in section \ref{sub:diagrams}. % Hier könnte eventuell etwas aus dem SOTA ausgelagert werden

Due to their structural complexity with heterogeneous data sources and multiple stakeholders involved, these artifacts are often large and difficult to interpret. Maintaining an overview can be especially challenging for junior-level enterprise architects. This challenge motivates the exploration of AI-supported solutions, which enable conversational interaction with the architecture rather than manual navigation of complex diagrams.

% Stakeholders
The research was conducted in close collaboration with the academic supervisor and the co-advisor. The academic supervisor gave academic guidance, supported the structuring of the research process to ensure academic relevance, and acted as an expert practitioner. The co-advisor acted as the domain expert and practitioner, contributing practical insights to ensure the research has real-world relevance. The author of this thesis assumed the role of the researcher, implementing the Action Research cycles, validating findings, and planning subsequent steps in coordination with the other stakeholders.

% Initial Problem Statement
At the outset of the research, the general problem was clear, but the potential solution was only vaguely defined. The co-advisor initiated the research with a vague vision of a centralized system containing all enterprise architecture information, which could be interacted with via natural language. The motivation for this was to reduce the effort required to interpret enterprise architecture artifacts and to offer alternative solutions to those available on the market.

However, in the early stages of the project, not only were the technical details of the potential solution unclear, but also the feasibility of such a system. Early on, it was mutually agreed upon that LLMs would play a key role in realizing this, even though the data structures, mechanisms, storage options, and interaction patterns were still open. Consequently, the initial problem statement was intentionally formulated at a high level to provide a suitable starting point for iterative exploration. Through iterative development cycles, this high-level problem statement, lacking a planned technical concept, was refined into a concrete problem definition, and a technical solution was developed.

It is important to note that during each cycle, continuous system tests were run by the researcher in order to ensure that the system's requirements were being met. For example, when adding new data to the knowledge graph, the system was prompted to test if it was able to retrieve this new data. In most cases, it was not able to do so right after adding new data. These tests led to the system's prompt needing to be continuously fine-tuned to handle the data in the knowledge graph. This was a routine step conducted regularly throughout development.

The development of the system was supported by generative AI tools such as ChatGPT. This was deemed appropriate as to accelerate progress in the prototype. \cite{openai_chatgpt_2023}
